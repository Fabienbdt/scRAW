\newcommand{\paperabstract}{Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data is essential for
describing tissue heterogeneity, but rare cell populations remain difficult to identify. This challenge affects
learning-based clustering methods because single-cell data are
high-dimensional, sparse, noisy, imbalanced, and often affected by
batch structure. In deep learning models, optimization is largely driven by
abundant cell types, such that rare cells often contribute too
weakly to organize the latent space. To address this, we present scRAW, a
rare-population autoencoder framework for scRNA-seq clustering. scRAW
combines standard preprocessing with a two-phase deep learning-based
training strategy. In the first phase, all cells contribute equally to the
reconstruction objective. In the second phase, cell-specific weights
are computed from latent pseudo-cluster size and local density such
that small cell groups contribute more strongly to training. A
triplet-based regularization then increases the saliency of
high-weight cells in the latent space, while adversarial batch correction
can be added when batch labels are available. The learned
representation is finally clustered with a state-of-the-art algorithm. We evaluated our approach on a human pancreas benchmark, where scRAW reached mean ARI
$=0.9005 \pm 0.0314$ and mean ACC $=0.9087 \pm 0.0215$ across 15
independent evaluations. scRAW also ranks first on several label-agreement, rare-population, internal-structure, and batch-effect metrics. These results support custom rare-cell reweighting as an effective framework-level strategy that improves rare-cell identification without degrading overall clustering quality.}

\section{Introduction}
% =============================================================================
scRNA-seq is used to study tissue heterogeneity, and clustering is a common way to identify cell populations in these data. Clustering scRNA-seq data is challenging because the gene expression matrix is high-dimensional, sparse, noisy, and often affected by batch effects \cite{tran2020}. Performance also depends on preprocessing, representation learning, and partitioning choices, leading to variability across datasets and methods \cite{kiselev2019,luecken2019,duo2018,freytag2018}. Many scRNA-seq clustering approaches still rely on conventional dimensionality-reduction and clustering pipelines. In particular, PCA representations are often combined with widely used clustering algorithms such as KMeans \cite{macqueen1967}, and PCA-based strategies have also been explored in methods such as pcaReduce \cite{zurauskiene2016}. Deep learning methods \cite{ren2022} follow the same general goal, but try to learn a latent space in which clustering becomes easier. In other words, the representation is optimized such that cells with similar profiles are brought closer together, while different populations are better separated before the final clustering step. These methods can then rely on different clustering strategies in the learned space, including KMeans-based objectives, graph-based clustering, or neighborhood-based contrastive clustering. Examples include scDeepCluster \cite{tian2019}, scMAE \cite{fang2024}, and scNAME \cite{wan2022}. This framing is consistent with the deep clustering literature, in which deep neural networks are used to learn clustering-friendly representations from complex data \cite{ren2022}.

However, in standard unsupervised training, the objective function is dominated by common cell types. As a result, neural networks tend to reach a local optimum optimized for predominant samples, leaving rare populations with a small influence on the learned representation. Consequently, rare cells are often absorbed into larger clusters and become difficult to identify, despite their biological significance \cite{grun2015}. While non-deep learning methods such as GiniClust \cite{jiang2016} and CellSIUS \cite{wegmann2019} were developed to improve rare-cell detection in single-cell data, they mainly operate as rare-population detection or post-clustering refinement tools. GiniClust is limited in preserving the global cluster structure, whereas CellSIUS depends on the quality of an initial fixed clustering.

To address this, we propose scRAW (single-cell Rare Autoencoder Weighting), a framework designed to reduce class imbalance in scRNA-seq by emphasizing rare populations during representation learning itself. Built around an autoencoder backbone, scRAW integrates adaptive cell weighting, local latent regularization, and optional batch correction. The framework is not restricted to a single reconstruction loss or clustering backend; rather, its goal is to make underrepresented cells more salient while preserving the global structure of the data.

\section{Materials and methods}
\subsection{scRAW Framework}
% =============================================================================
\subsubsection*{Overview}

The scRAW framework has four steps: preprocessing of the gene expression matrix, learning a latent space with an autoencoder (AE), periodic updates of cell weights, and final clustering in the latent space. The model is a deep autoencoder with symmetric encoder and decoder. An adversarial batch head is connected to the latent space and trained with the DANN criterion \cite{ganin2016} to limit technical batch information.


\subsubsection*{Preprocessing}

Before training, the gene expression matrix is processed with a standard
Scanpy-based pipeline \cite{wolf2018,luecken2019}. For the experiments reported here, cells with
fewer than 200 detected genes are removed, and genes detected in fewer
than 3 cells are discarded. Counts were normalized per cell to a fixed total of 20,000 in order to place all cells on a common scale before log transformation
with $\log(1+x)$. We then retain the top 2,000 highly variable genes
using the Seurat flavor \cite{satija2015}. Finally, each gene is standardized across
cells with a gene-wise z-score and clipped to the range $[-10, 10]$ for
numerical stability \cite{luecken2019}. No separate batch correction is applied at the
preprocessing stage; when used, batch handling is introduced later by
the adversarial branch. The resulting dense standardized matrix is used
as the input to the autoencoder and, in this study, as the
reconstruction target.

\subsubsection*{Reconstruction and Training Procedure}

Let $X \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times G}$ denote the
input gene-expression matrix used by the autoencoder, where $X_{c,g}$
is the preprocessed expression value of gene $g$ in cell $c$, with $N$
cells and $G$ genes. Let $\hat{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times G}$ denote
the reconstructed matrix, and
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{recon},i}$ denotes the reconstruction
error on cell $i$. The reconstruction loss is the mean squared error (MSE), defined for cell $i$ as
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{recon},i} = \lVert \hat{X}_i - X_i \rVert^2$.
A fraction of genes is randomly masked from the input, following the general idea of masked autoencoder approaches for single-cell data \cite{fang2024,richter2025}. Training is organized in two phases. In phase 1, the latent space is stabilized with uniform reconstruction weights. In phase 2, dynamic weighting, triplet loss, and, when multiple batches are present, adversarial correction are activated. When masking is applied, masked entries can receive a larger coefficient inside this reconstruction term. We define the dynamic weighted reconstruction loss as follows:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{recon,weighted}}
  =
  \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \omega_i\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{recon},i}
\end{equation}
where $\omega_i$ is the final reconstruction weight assigned to cell
$i$ (see \autoref{eq:omega_weight}).
The total optimized loss is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{total}}
  =
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{recon,weighted}}
  +
  \rho(e)\,\lambda_{\mathrm{triplet}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{triplet}}
  +
  \lambda_{\mathrm{batch}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{batch}}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{triplet}}$ is the local constraint in the
latent space \cite{schroff2015,yang2016}, $\rho(e)$ is the triplet ramp-up term, and
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{batch}}$ is activated using the DANN criterion
\cite{ganin2016} for adversarial batch correction.
The weights assigned to the triplet and adversarial batch terms can be
adapted depending on the user and dataset. 



\subsubsection*{Rare-Cell MSE Weighting and Triplet Loss}

In phase 1, all cells contribute equally, i.e. $\omega_i = 1$. In the
benchmark setting reported here, phase 1 lasts $74$ epochs before
phase 2 begins. In phase 2, each cell receives a dynamic weight $\omega_i$ that determines
how much it contributes to the reconstruction loss. A large $\omega_i$
gives a cell more influence during training, whereas a small
$\omega_i$ reduces its effect. In the additive weighting scheme used here,
$\omega_i$ combines a cluster-size term and a local-density term.
In the benchmark setting reported here, pseudo-labels and cell weights
are updated every 10 epochs. At each update, Leiden is rerun on the
current latent space. In this study, the Leiden resolution is also
reselected at each update by a grid search that targets 14 clusters and
maximizes ARI against the reference labels.

The cluster-size term is computed from Leiden pseudo-clusters in the
current latent space,
\begin{equation}
  \omega^{\mathrm{cluster}}_i
  =
  \left( \frac{1}{\lvert C_{\ell_i} \rvert} \right)^{\gamma_{\mathrm{cluster}}}
\end{equation}
where $C_{\ell_i}$ is the pseudo-cluster assigned to cell $i$,
$\lvert C_{\ell_i} \rvert$ is the number of cells in that
pseudo-cluster, and $\gamma_{\mathrm{cluster}}$ controls how strongly
small pseudo-clusters are upweighted. In simple terms, cells from
smaller pseudo-clusters receive larger $\omega^{\mathrm{cluster}}_i$
values.

The local-density term is estimated from latent
$k$-nearest-neighbor distances. Let $\mathcal{N}_k(i)$ denote the set
of the $k$ nearest neighbors of cell $i$ in the latent space, and let
$D_i = \{d(i,j) \mid j \in \mathcal{N}_k(i)\}$ be the corresponding set
of Euclidean distances, with $k=15$ in the benchmark experiments reported
here. We
summarize this neighborhood by $\max D_i$, which here is the distance
to the $k$-th nearest neighbor. A larger $\max D_i$ means that the cell
is more isolated, while a smaller $\max D_i$ means that it lies in a
denser region. The density-based weight is then
\begin{equation}
  \omega^{\mathrm{dens}}_i
  =
  \left(
    \frac{\max D_i}{\mathrm{median}_{m=1,\ldots,N}\, \max D_m}
  \right)^{\gamma_{\mathrm{dens}}}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma_{\mathrm{dens}}$ is the density exponent, and
$\mathrm{median}_{m=1,\ldots,N}\, \max D_m$ is the median distance to
the $k$-th nearest neighbor computed across all cells. In other words,
the distance to the $k$-th nearest neighbor of cell $i$ is normalized
by the median of the same quantity across the whole dataset. As a
result, cells located in sparse latent
regions receive larger $\omega^{\mathrm{dens}}_i$ values, while cells
embedded in denser neighborhoods receive smaller ones.

The final reconstruction weight is obtained by combining both terms,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:omega_weight}
  \omega_i
  =
  \alpha\,\omega^{\mathrm{cluster}}_i
  +
  (1-\alpha)\,\omega^{\mathrm{dens}}_i
\end{equation}
where $\alpha \in [0,1]$ controls the trade-off between pseudo-cluster
size and local isolation. In the benchmark experiments reported here, $\alpha=0.3$.
The resulting weights are
rescaled for numerical stability before being used in the loss. Accordingly, $\omega_i$ is large when a cell belongs to a small
pseudo-cluster, lies
in a sparse neighborhood, or both.

In practice, the reconstruction loss is the main global training signal. Cells
with larger $\omega_i$ have more influence on the reconstruction term,
such that the encoder and decoder devote more capacity to rare cell expression
profiles. Common cells still contribute, but with a smaller effect on
the training.

Triplet loss adds a second signal in the latent space and has been used
both in deep metric learning and in clustering-oriented representation
learning \cite{schroff2015,yang2016}. High-weight
cells are used as anchors, then paired with a positive cell from the
same pseudo-group and a negative cell from another group. This term
does not improve reconstruction directly. Instead, it makes rare groups
more salient locally and separates them better from nearby larger
groups in the latent space.

The two terms are therefore complementary: weighted MSE helps preserve
the expression pattern of rare cells, while triplet loss improves their
saliency in the latent space.

At the end of training, clustering is performed directly in the latent space with HDBSCAN \cite{mcinnes2017}.

\subsection{Experimental Setting}
% =============================================================================



\subsubsection*{Dataset}

We evaluate scRAW on the Human Baron Pancreas dataset \cite{baron2016}, a widely used single-cell RNA-seq benchmark for pancreatic cell-type identification. In the benchmark file used here, Baron contains $8569$ cells annotated into $14$ cell types and organized into $4$ batches: \textit{human1} ($1937$ cells), \textit{human2} ($1724$), \textit{human3} ($3605$), and \textit{human4} ($1303$). For the experiments reported here, preprocessing retains the top $2000$ highly variable genes as model input. The dataset spans both abundant and rare populations (see \autoref{tab:rare_population_compare}), which makes it suitable for evaluating overall clustering quality as well as rare-cell identification. In this work, Baron is treated as a batch-aware benchmark; therefore, the evaluation considers both biological label agreement and the ability to reduce residual batch effects in the learned embedding. 

\subsubsection*{Compared Methods}

For benchmarking, we compare scRAW with two conventional baselines and
three published deep learning baselines. The conventional baselines are
PCA+Leiden, using Leiden community detection \cite{traag2019} after PCA
projection, and PCA+KMeans, using the widely used KMeans algorithm
\cite{macqueen1967} after PCA projection. For both baselines, the number
of PCA components was selected automatically with Cattell's scree test.
For PCA+Leiden, the Leiden resolution was selected automatically through
a silhouette-based search on a predefined
grid. The number of predicted clusters was therefore induced by this
resolution and was not fixed in advance. For PCA+KMeans, the number of
clusters was set to the number of reference labels in Baron
($K = 14$). The same choice was also used for the compared KMeans-based
deep baselines when their implementations required $K$ to be specified
in advance.
The deep learning baselines
are scDeepCluster \cite{tian2019}, scMAE \cite{fang2024}, and scNAME
\cite{wan2022}. 
%These methods were evaluated with their standard or default settings,
%whereas the reported scRAW results correspond to the Optuna-selected
%hyperparameter setting described below.
For these methods, we use the default or recommended hyperparameter settings provided in their original implementations or publications. These settings were also used when evaluating the methods on the Baron dataset in their original studies. For scRAW, results correspond to the Optuna-selected hyperparameter setting described below.

\subsubsection*{Evaluation Metrics}

We evaluated performance with four metric groups: label-agreement, rare-population, internal-structure, and
batch-effect. Label-agreement metrics were computed by
comparing predicted clusters with reference cell-type labels after
excluding cells marked as noise. ARI \cite{hubert1985} and NMI
\cite{vinh2010} measure the agreement between the predicted partition
and the reference partition without relying on cluster names. ACC and
the mean unweighted F1 score \cite{sokolova2009}, as well as balanced accuracy
\cite{brodersen2010}, were computed after Hungarian matching between
predicted clusters and reference labels, such that each cluster was
associated with the most consistent label.

Because rare populations are a main target of scRAW, we also report
rare-population metrics. RareACC is the accuracy restricted to classes
representing less than $5\%$ of the evaluated cells. On Baron, this
$5\%$ threshold corresponds to fewer than $429$ evaluated cells per
class. We additionally report UltraRareACC, defined as the accuracy
restricted to cell types with at most $30$ evaluated cells per class.
On Baron, this threshold corresponds to approximately $0.35\%$ of the
$8569$ evaluated cells. The ultra-rare classes are
\textit{t\_cell} ($7$ cells), \textit{schwann} ($13$),
\textit{epsilon} ($18$), and \textit{mast} ($25$); together, these
four classes account for $63$ evaluated cells in total. As an internal-structure
metric, we also report the silhouette score to summarize how coherent
the predicted clusters are and how well they are separated in the
embedding space.

These metrics capture complementary aspects of performance. ARI and NMI mainly summarize the global quality of the partition and are invariant to the arbitrary numbering of clusters. ACC is easier to interpret as a fraction of correctly assigned cells after label matching, but it is still influenced by the largest populations. The mean unweighted F1 score and balanced accuracy reduce this dominance by giving each class a more even role in the final score. Here, the mean unweighted F1 score is the arithmetic mean of the class-wise F1 scores after Hungarian matching, without weighting classes by their support. In practice, this metric is sensitive to both low precision and low recall on a given class, while balanced accuracy is the mean class-wise recall after matching.

RareACC and UltraRareACC should be interpreted as subset accuracies
rather than macro-averages over rare classes. They count all cells
belonging to the selected rare groups and compute the fraction of
correctly assigned cells inside that subset. As a result, larger rare
groups contribute more than very small ones, and UltraRareACC was added
to isolate the hardest cell types in Baron. For this reason, we also
report per-cell-type recall in the rare-population analysis table,
which makes it possible to see which specific rare or ultra-rare
populations are identified or missed. The silhouette score provides a
different perspective as an internal-structure metric: it depends only
on the predicted clustering and the structure of the latent space, and indicates whether
predicted clusters form coherent, well-separated groups, but it does not by itself
guarantee agreement with biological labels.

For batch-effect evaluation, we report the final aggregated scIB-E score following the benchmarking framework proposed by Yi et al. \cite{yi2025}. This score is designed to measure batch-effect correction by combining batch-mixing and biological-conservation criteria into a single summary: higher values indicate that technical batch structure is better reduced while biologically meaningful variation is preserved.


\subsubsection*{Hyperparameter Search}
In the experiments reported
here, we set $\lambda_{\mathrm{triplet}} = 0.2346$ and
$\lambda_{\mathrm{batch}} = 0.0562$.
The hyperparameters used in this work were selected with Optuna
\cite{akiba2019}.
We used a two-stage procedure consisting of a broad Optuna TPE search followed by repeated reruns of the top-ranked hyperparameter settings. In stage 1, Optuna explored 450 hyperparameter settings on the Baron human pancreas dataset, with one full run per trial, TPE sampling, and 32 startup trials before adaptive model-based proposals; DANN was enabled throughout and the target number of clusters was fixed to 14. In stage 2, we kept the top 10 settings from stage 1 and reran each of them with three independent seeds, yielding 30 additional full runs and 480 training/evaluation runs overall. This second pass was used to reduce seed-specific variance: candidates were re-ranked by the mean of the same weighted clustering score across the three reruns, and the final selected setting was the one with the best refined average score. The selection score combined ARI, NMI, the mean unweighted F1 score, balanced accuracy, and rare-class accuracy.

\section{Results and Discussion}

\subsection{Overall Performance}

As shown in \autoref{tab:benchmark_compare}, scRAW provides the strongest overall performance on the global label-agreement metrics, on UltraRareACC, and on the aggregated scIB-E score. Among the baselines, PCA+Leiden is the strongest on ARI, NMI, and ACC, PCA+KMeans gives the highest UltraRareACC, scMAE gives the strongest mean unweighted F1 score and BalancedACC, scDeepCluster gives the best RareACC, and scNAME gives the highest Silhouette.

Among the baseline methods, PCA+Leiden is the best performer on
the global agreement metrics. This suggests
that identifying the large dominant populations already gives a
strong advantage on ARI, NMI, and ACC. Because these metrics are influenced by the largest classes, strong performance on the dominant populations can partly mask weaker identification of rare cell types. PCA+KMeans does not match
PCA+Leiden on these global metrics, but it performs better on
UltraRareACC, which shows that a simple PCA-based pipeline can still
identify part of the smallest cell groups. Across seed reruns, PCA+Leiden is also markedly less stable than PCA+KMeans, which is consistent with the stronger sensitivity of graph-based clustering to seed-dependent changes in the neighborhood graph and Leiden partition. Among the deep learning baselines, scMAE performs best on the class-balanced global metrics,
scDeepCluster achieves the strongest RareACC, and scNAME provides the most
regular latent-space structure. There is therefore
no single baseline that dominates across all metrics.

Concerning the batch effect correction, scRAW gives the strongest scIB-E result overall, followed by scNAME. PCA+Leiden and PCA+KMeans are then nearly tied, slightly ahead of scMAE, whereas scDeepCluster is clearly lower. This indicates that the learned scRAW representation provides the best overall trade-off between batch correction and biological conservation among the methods considered here.

\subsection{Rare Population Detection}

Table~\ref{tab:rare_population_compare} reports the per-cell-type accuracy, defined here as class-wise recall, for the best-performing evaluation of each algorithm. The representative evaluation for each method was selected by BalancedACC across the 15 seed reruns. Among the baselines, identification is more partial and fragmented: PCA+KMeans and scMAE identify \textit{mast} cells well, scMAE and scNAME identify the \textit{t\_cell} group in their selected runs, PCA+KMeans also identifies a large part of the \textit{schwann} population, and scDeepCluster fully identifies \textit{gamma} while still capturing only part of \textit{schwann}. None of these baselines matches the overall coverage of scRAW across the smallest groups. On the broader set of rare populations, the pattern is more heterogeneous, with different baselines performing better on different cell types. In the best-performing scRAW evaluation, the only remaining extra cluster is limited in size and appears to correspond to a local split of the ductal compartment rather than to an additional distinct population. It contains $171$ cells, mostly ductal ($156$ cells), with a small number of acinar ($13$) and beta ($2$) cells. Under Hungarian matching, predicted clusters are assigned to reference labels in a one-to-one manner. This small mixed cluster remains unmatched because the ductal label is already assigned to a larger predicted cluster, and the remaining cells do not match another reference label clearly enough. This reflects a limitation of the Hungarian algorithm, which favors a single best one-to-one correspondence even when a biological population is split into two predicted clusters. This suggests a local over-segmentation of one region rather than a systematic failure across cell types.

Figure~\ref{fig:umaps} shows the clustering results for all compared methods. In scRAW, the predicted clusters match the ground-truth labels well, and the misclassification panel shows that errors are few and mostly affect the rarest cell types. The cell rarity panel shows that high-$\omega$ cells, that is, cells from small pseudo-clusters or sparse latent regions, correspond to the small isolated groups visible in the UMAP, which are the ultra-rare populations that scRAW targets. PCA+Leiden separates the main populations correctly but does not resolve the smallest groups, consistent with its zero score on UltraRareACC in Table~\ref{tab:benchmark_compare}. PCA+KMeans identifies part of the ultra-rare classes, but still misses a substantial fraction of them, consistent with its intermediate performance on UltraRareACC. Among the deep learning baselines, scMAE produces an embedding in which part of the ultra-rare signal becomes salient, but still misses several rare groups; scDeepCluster identifies more rare cells overall but its latent space is fragmented, as reflected by its low silhouette score; scNAME produces the most regular latent-space structure but does not identify the smallest populations consistently across runs. Overall, the UMAP panels show that scRAW is the only method that both preserves the global cluster structure and identifies the ultra-rare populations emphasized by the rarity weights.

\subsection{Ablation Study}

Table~\ref{tab:ablation_trial206} summarizes the ablation experiments around the selected MSE-based setup, including both weighted and plain MSE baselines. The strongest variant depends on the metric: weighted MSE + triplet gives the highest ARI and NMI, whereas the full weighted MSE + DANN + triplet variant, which corresponds to the scRAW model used in this study, gives the highest ACC, mean F1 score, BalancedACC, and RareACC. For batch correction alone, the highest scIB-E score is obtained by the single-update variant ($0.5882$), slightly ahead of weighted MSE + DANN ($0.5867$). This indicates that stronger batch mixing does not automatically translate into better clustering quality. The plain MSE-only autoencoder is by far the weakest baseline, which indicates that the observed gains are not explained by the backbone alone. The weighted MSE-only variant is already much stronger than this plain baseline, showing that phase-2 reweighting is beneficial even without DANN or triplet loss. Among the weighted variants, DANN without triplet remains clearly suboptimal on the label-agreement metrics despite its high scIB-E score. Collapsing the dynamic-weight updates to a single update, or suppressing either the cluster-size or density component of the weighting all degrade performance relative to the full model, with the density component having a particularly large effect on ARI and NMI.

\section{Conclusion and Perspectives}

On the Baron benchmark, scRAW gives the strongest overall results on
the global label-agreement metrics, on UltraRareACC, and on the
aggregated scIB-E score. These results indicate that the method can
preserve the main cluster structure while improving the identification of the
smallest cell populations and maintaining a favorable trade-off between
biological conservation and batch correction. The ablation study also
shows that the gains do not come from the autoencoder backbone alone:
the phase-2 weighting is already important, and the combination of
weighted reconstruction, triplet regularization, and adversarial batch
correction gives the strongest overall variant.

These findings should nevertheless be validated beyond Baron.
The method should be tested on additional datasets to determine
whether the same trends hold beyond Baron. For example, the OpenProblems
pancreas dataset \cite{openproblems_pancreas} could provide a useful benchmark to assess scRAW under
stronger batch effects. It would also be relevant to evaluate the model
in a generalization setting by testing it on data not seen during
training.
